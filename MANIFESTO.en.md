**Owning the structure of your expertise, in the age of AI.**

---

Generative AI changes a great deal, except one thing: it still does not know what you know. Your clients, your constraints, your risk threshold, your way of deciding, the history of your files: none of that lives in a model. A model masters countless domains, yet nothing of your reality: by default, it does not carry its memory from one session to the next, and the language that drives it stays underspecified, which is at once its flexibility and its fragility. If nothing is written down, it improvises. If everything is scattered across interfaces, it becomes impossible to maintain.

The reflex inherited from the digital world is to push this collaboration into closed platforms: hand-configured agents, instructions scattered across several screens, dispersed permissions, agent choices to keep in your head. You produce faster, but you no longer know what guided the production, what was verified, what remains reusable, or what stays under human responsibility. AI then increases the mental load instead of reducing it.

BASE takes the opposite path.

The tipping point is simple. Producing has become nearly effortless; verifying remains costly. And for most real work, no external verifier exists: no compiler, no schema, no rulebook. It is the human who must detect and correct the errors, and judge whether an output truly serves their intent, calibrated to the risk they accept. Everything else in this manifesto follows from that: keeping the human **able to verify**, without drowning them, and over time.

## What we hold to be true

1. **The durable value is neither the model nor the tool: it is the structure of your expertise.** Models change every six months; your business files, your processes, your rules and your decisions must remain yours.
2. **Text is the truth.** Readable, versionable, portable Markdown. Not a proprietary database, not a format you can neither take with you nor audit.
3. **Generating takes little effort; verifying takes much.** Every AI output is to be treated as a hypothesis, not a settled conclusion. A strong structure makes verification light, never the other way around.
4. **A guarantee is only real if it is enforced.** We honestly distinguish what is an instruction (text, followed by a cooperative model) from what is a mechanism (enforced by the broker). We document the boundary instead of papering over it.
5. **The important decision stays human and visible.** Decision points, markers and the journal keep control and memory on the side of people.
6. **Sovereignty is not negotiable.** The local core of BASE sends nothing out by default. If you plug in an AI tool, an embeddings provider or an API, you choose what is entrusted to them. You remain free to switch tools; your files remain. The sovereignty that matters lies **around the models**: the freedom to articulate, to structure, to think with these intelligences.
7. **What matters must be visible.** You should never have to dig to encounter the essential. BASE puts forward what needs verifying or deciding, orients rather than leaving you hanging, and keeps help within a word's reach. Burying what matters is already a small loss of verification.

## What we do not promise

BASE does not make AI infallible. It replaces neither IAM, nor DLP, nor legal archiving, nor the governance of a large organization. It imposes no model, no provider, no interface. It does not claim that everything must be automated, nor that AI possesses guaranteed intent or understanding.

Our ambition is **modest in its promises and strong in its method**: making human-AI collaboration more structured, more verifiable and more durable. The strength does not come from a promised reliability, but from the structure of the interaction and its safeguards: iterate, refocus, explain, and keep human presence at the points that matter, to validate according to the accepted risk.

## Why it matters, at every scale

What holds for a freelancer holds, at larger scale, for a team, a school or a public administration. Real progress does not consist in delegating judgment, but in **structuring the relationship**: writing down what the system must know, bounding what it may do, keeping visible the decisions that commit you. You can then move forward with AI **without depending on a single platform**, and without giving up the structure of your work.

In a multilingual country, it is also a matter of linguistic comfort. The core of BASE is language-independent: the assistants you build work in German, French, Italian or English, in the language of those who use them. The framework's documentation starts in French; its translations are a commons to enrich.
